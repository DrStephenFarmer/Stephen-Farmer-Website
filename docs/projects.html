Projects

The projects below form part of a broader translational research programme aimed at developing scientifically grounded methods for estimating eyewitness reliability and translating those findings into evidence-based guidance, training, policy, and practice within the justice system.

Each project contributes both to scientific understanding and to the development of practical resources capable of improving the evaluation of eyewitness evidence.

Current Research

Completed

Eyewitness Forgetting and Retention Interval Modelling

Roadmap Stage
Witnessing Conditions

Research Question
How does eyewitness accuracy change over time?

Overview
This project investigates how retention interval influences eyewitness identification accuracy across timescales ranging from minutes to months.

Contribution
Provides foundational evidence regarding how memory decay influences eyewitness reliability.

Potential Outputs

  • Police training resources
  • Expert witness evidence base
  • Public-facing resources on eyewitness memory
  • Evidence-based guidance regarding delayed eyewitness evidence

Ongoing

Estimating Eyewitness Identification Accuracy Across Witnessing Conditions

Roadmap Stage
Predictive Reliability Estimation

Research Question
Can eyewitness identification accuracy be estimated probabilistically across varying witnessing conditions?

Overview
This project develops calibrated probabilistic models of eyewitness identification accuracy across conditions including retention interval, separation distance, and exposure duration.

Contribution
Provides the probabilistic estimation framework that underpins the broader goal of computational estimation of eyewitness reliability.

Potential Outputs

  • Reliability tables
  • Practitioner guidance
  • Investigative decision-support resources
  • Policy-relevant evidence for evaluating eyewitness evidence

Ongoing

Evidence Accumulation and Decision Processes in Eyewitness Identification

Roadmap Stage
Evidence Accumulation and Decision Processes

Research Question
How do eyewitnesses accumulate and evaluate evidence during identification decisions?

Overview
This project applies computational models of decision-making to eyewitness identification in order to understand how witnessing conditions, confidence, and response time influence evidence accumulation and identification accuracy.

Current work uses drift diffusion modelling as a framework for investigating evidence diagnosticity and decision quality.

Contribution
Provides a mechanistic account of eyewitness decision-making and links witnessing conditions to identification outcomes through evidence accumulation processes.

Potential Outputs

  • Mechanistic explanations of eyewitness decisions
  • Improved predictive models
  • Open-source modelling resources
  • Computational research infrastructure

Ongoing

Confidence and Metacognition in Eyewitness Identification

Roadmap Stage
Decision Indicators

Research Question
When and why does confidence predict eyewitness accuracy?

Overview
This project investigates confidence calibration, confidence diagnosticity, and metacognitive monitoring under varying witnessing conditions.

Contribution
Examines how witnesses evaluate their own memory and when confidence can meaningfully inform reliability assessments.

Potential Outputs

  • Confidence interpretation guidance
  • Police interview guidance
  • Judicial education resources
  • Expert witness resources

Ongoing

Predictive Reliability Estimation

Roadmap Stage
Predictive Reliability Estimation

Research Question
Can eyewitness reliability be estimated using calibrated predictive models that integrate witnessing conditions and decision indicators?

Overview
This project integrates findings from witnessing conditions, confidence, response time, and computational modelling to develop transparent and calibrated estimates of eyewitness reliability.

Contribution
Forms the direct pathway toward practical reliability estimation tools capable of supporting evidence evaluation within legal settings.

Potential Outputs

  • Reliability estimation frameworks
  • Practitioner guidance
  • Investigative decision-support resources
  • Foundations for courtroom applications

Ongoing

Juror Decision-Making and Communication of Eyewitness Reliability

Roadmap Stage
Translation to Justice

Research Question
How can eyewitness reliability and uncertainty be communicated effectively to legal decision-makers?

Overview
This research theme examines how jurors evaluate eyewitness evidence, how confidence and reliability information influence judgments, and how scientific evidence regarding eyewitness accuracy can be communicated effectively within legal settings.

Current work includes the evaluation of the Turnbull safeguard and broader investigations of juror reasoning, evidence interpretation, and decision-making.

Contribution
Provides the bridge between computational estimation of eyewitness reliability and its practical application within the justice system.

Potential Outputs

  • Jury education resources
  • Judicial guidance
  • Communication frameworks
  • Policy recommendations

Programme Integration

Together, these projects form a coherent translational research programme progressing from witnessing conditions and decision indicators, through evidence accumulation and predictive modelling, towards ecological validation and the communication of reliability estimates within legal settings.

The long-term objective is to develop scientifically grounded and transparent methods for estimating the probability that an eyewitness identification is correct and to translate those methods into practical guidance, training, policy, and decision-support resources capable of improving the evaluation of eyewitness evidence within the justice system.
